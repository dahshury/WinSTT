Press your hotkey, speak, and WinSTT pastes the polished text straight into whatever you
were typing in — a chat box, an editor, a terminal, a search bar. Everything between the
trigger and the paste happens on your machine.

## The dictation loop

Whatever starts a recording — a held key, a tapped key, your speakers, or a spoken
keyword — every mode runs the same four steps. Only the **start trigger** differs; the
transcribe → optional cleanup → paste tail is identical.

<Video
  src="dictation-loop"
  chrome="card"
  alt="A WinSTT workflow diagram showing the trigger, live preview, final accurate pass, and paste delivery at the cursor."
  label="The dictation loop"
  caption="Every mode shares the same tail: preview, final pass, optional cleanup, paste."
/>

<StepFlow>
  <Step title="Trigger a recording">
    Hold your **Push-to-Talk** key, **tap** the toggle key, **say** the wake word,
    or let **Listen** mode capture system audio. A short chime confirms the start
    (off in Listen / Wake Word modes), and the recording overlay appears.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Speak — watch the live preview">
    A small, fast realtime model streams a rough preview of your words as you
    talk, so you can see it's hearing you. This preview is *not* the final text
    — it's a progress indicator. It can show in the app window, the overlay
    pill, both, or nowhere (see
    <a href="#live-transcription-display">Live-transcription display</a>).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Stop">
    **Release** the key (Push-to-Talk), **press again** (Toggle), or just **stop
    talking** — the Smart Endpoint and post-speech silence timer end the
    utterance automatically after `0.7s` of silence (default). The overlay shows
    a brief "thinking" state while the main, accurate model produces the final
    transcription.
  </Step>
  <Step title="(Optional) LLM cleanup, then paste">
    If you've enabled [LLM cleanup](/docs/settings/quality#llm-cleanup), the final text runs
    through your chosen modifier (tone, translate, custom) before delivery.
    WinSTT then drops the result at your cursor — and optionally presses Enter
    to submit.
  </Step>
</StepFlow>

<Callout type="info" title="Cancel mid-flight">
  Press <Kbd>Escape</Kbd> to abort an in-progress recording, the transcription,
  or the LLM pass. Hold the hotkey and tap <Kbd>↑</Kbd> to cycle the recording
  mode without opening settings.
</Callout>

## The four recording modes

A recording mode decides **what starts a recording** — a held key, a tapped key, your
speakers, or a spoken keyword. All four share a single pipeline and **one paste
endpoint**, so switching modes never changes transcription quality, only the way you kick
it off. Set the mode under [General Settings](/docs/settings/general).

<SettingRow label="Recording Mode" settingKey="general.recordingMode" default="ptt">
  Selects the trigger strategy: **Push-to-Talk**, **Toggle**, **Listen**, or **Wake
  Word**. The mode only governs the start (and, for some modes, the stop) trigger — the
  downstream transcription and paste are identical across all four.
</SettingRow>

### Push-to-Talk

<ModeDemo
  mode="ptt"
  caption="Hold to record, release to transcribe — the clip lasts exactly as long as you hold the key."
/>

The default mode, and the most predictable. **Hold** the hotkey, speak, **release** to
stop — the recording lasts exactly as long as you hold the key. There's no silence
detection on the stop side, so a release triggers an immediate, fast finalize. One press
authorizes exactly one recording, then clears.

Push-to-Talk is also the only mode that **bypasses the speech-onset debounce** (see
[below](#why-the-other-modes-wait-a-beat)) — because holding the key already declares
intent, there's no noise to gate.

<SettingRow label="Push-to-Talk Hotkey" settingKey="hotkey.pushToTalkKey" default="LCtrl+LMeta">
  The key (or combo) you hold to record. Captured via the global keyboard hook, so it
  works in any focused app. Change it on the [Hotkeys](/docs/settings/hotkey) page; it
  updates at runtime without a restart.
</SettingRow>

**Best for:** quick bursts, voice commands, and noisy rooms where automatic voice
detection would false-trigger.

### Toggle

<ModeDemo
  mode="toggle"
  caption="Tap once to open a hands-free session that records turn after turn; tap again to close it."
/>

Tap the hotkey once to open a **continuous session** and tap again to close it — between
those two taps, WinSTT records utterance after utterance hands-free. After you stop
talking it finalizes the turn on silence; when you start again it opens the next recording
automatically. The recording pill stays up for the whole session, not just the first
sentence.

<SettingRow label="Manual Toggle Stop" settingKey="general.manualToggleStop" default="false">
  When on, **disables** silence-based endpoint detection so the session records
  continuously from the first tap to the second — nothing stops a turn but you. Good for
  long-form dictation with long natural pauses; with it off, each pause ends a turn.
</SettingRow>

**Best for:** hands-free long-form dictation, dictating with natural pauses, or capturing
a stretch of speech without holding a key.

### Listen

<ModeDemo
  mode="listen"
  caption="No hotkey — system audio streams into a rolling, optionally speaker-labeled subtitle feed."
/>

Listen mode captures **system audio** — what comes out of your speakers — over a WASAPI
loopback device, then streams a rolling, continuously-transcribed subtitle feed. There's
no hotkey; it runs as soon as the mode is active and a loopback device is selected. It uses
the **realtime model only**, and caps each utterance at 15 seconds so the feed never stalls
on a long monologue.

<Screenshot src="settings-general-listen" alt="The General tab in Listen mode, showing the loopback device picker and the Speaker Diarization toggle that replace the microphone controls." label="Settings — General (Listen)" chrome="card" caption="Microphone controls swap for a loopback picker and a diarization toggle." />

<SettingRow label="Loopback Device" settingKey="general.loopbackDeviceIndex" default="null" startupOnly>
  The WASAPI loopback (system-audio) device to capture from. You need a loopback source
  available — many sound cards expose one, or install a virtual cable such as VB-Audio
  Virtual Cable. The standard microphone **Input Device** picker is hidden in this mode.
</SettingRow>

<SettingRow label="Speaker Diarization" settingKey="general.speakerDiarization" default="false">
  Labels each line of the subtitle feed with a speaker (Speaker 1, Speaker 2…). Toggles
  **at runtime** — no restart, and it even applies to an in-flight session.
</SettingRow>

<Callout type="warn" title="A loopback device is required">
  Listen mode can't start without a loopback source. If none is available, the device
  picker is empty and the mode has nothing to capture — pick a real microphone mode or
  install a virtual cable first. The loopback device is read at startup, so changing it
  needs a server restart.
</Callout>

**Best for:** transcribing meetings, calls, videos, and anything else playing through your
speakers.

### Wake Word

<ModeDemo
  mode="wakeword"
  caption="Fully hands-free — say the keyword (Alexa, Computer, Jarvis…) and it arms a recording instantly."
/>

Wake Word mode listens continuously for a spoken keyword and starts recording the instant
it hears one — fully hands-free, no hotkey. After detection it records a turn and ends it
on silence (the same way Toggle does), then goes back to waiting for the keyword. WinSTT
picks the detection backend automatically (a composite of **Porcupine** and
**openWakeWord**) based on the chosen keyword.

<Screenshot src="settings-general-wakeword" alt="The General tab in Wake Word mode, showing the wake-word selector, sensitivity slider, and detection-timeout controls." label="Settings — General (Wake Word)" chrome="card" caption="Exposes the keyword, sensitivity, and a post-trigger timeout." />

<SettingRow label="Wake Word" settingKey="general.wakeWord" default="alexa">
  The keyword that arms a recording — for example **Alexa**, **Computer**, **Jarvis**, or
  **Hey Google**. The right backend (Porcupine or openWakeWord) is selected automatically
  for the chosen word.
</SettingRow>

<SettingRow label="Wake Word Sensitivity" settingKey="general.wakeWordSensitivity" default="0.6" startupOnly>
  How eager the detector is, from 0 to 1. Higher catches the keyword more reliably but
  raises false triggers; lower is stricter. Each engine clamps the value internally.
</SettingRow>

<SettingRow label="Wake Word Timeout" settingKey="general.wakeWordTimeout" default="5" startupOnly>
  Seconds (1–30) the recording stays armed after the keyword fires before it gives up and
  returns to listening. This is independent of the per-utterance silence timer.
</SettingRow>

<Callout type="warn" title="Wake-word settings apply at startup">
  The keyword, its sensitivity, and the timeout are read when the detection engine loads.
  Changing any of them needs a server restart to take effect.
</Callout>

**Best for:** fully hands-free, always-on voice activation across the room.

## Why the other modes wait a beat

Toggle, Listen, and Wake Word all start a turn from *audio*, so a cough or a door slam
could pop the pill and wake the model for nothing. To prevent that, those modes gate the
start behind a short **speech-onset debounce** — the pipeline waits for a few consecutive
speech chunks (default **3**) before it commits to a recording. **Push-to-Talk skips this
gate entirely**, since holding the key is already an explicit signal of intent.

The post-speech silence window then governs how long a pause must run before a turn ends —
but only for the modes that stop on silence:

<ModelTable
  head={["Mode", "Silence timing role"]}
  rows={[
    ["Push-to-Talk", "Unused. Releasing the key triggers an immediate, fast stop."],
    ["Toggle", "Active — ends each turn after the silence window. Disabled when Manual Toggle Stop is on."],
    ["Listen", "Active — segments the continuous loopback feed into turns."],
    ["Wake Word", "Active after the keyword fires — ends the turn the same way Toggle does."],
  ]}
  caption="Tune the window itself in Audio settings; when Smart Endpoint is on, its classifier supersedes the fixed timers."
/>

Lower silence values make WinSTT react faster to pauses but can clip speech with natural
breaks; higher values tolerate longer pauses at the cost of a later finalize. When
[Smart Endpoint](/docs/settings/quality) is enabled, a sentence classifier decides when a
turn is complete instead of the fixed timers.

## After the paste

WinSTT drops the finished text at your cursor as one block — the whole sentence appears at
once, the way modern dictation tools behave. Two optional behaviors round out delivery.

### Auto-submit after paste

Found under **Settings → General → Paste Behavior**. Off by default to preserve the
classic "paste and let me review" behavior.

<AutoSubmitDemo caption="The dictated message lands in the chat field, then WinSTT presses Enter — and it sends on its own." />

<SettingRow
  label="Auto-Submit After Paste"
  settingKey="general.autoSubmit"
  default="false"
>
  When on, WinSTT injects a submit key right after each successful paste — so a
  dictated chat message ships without a separate Enter press.
</SettingRow>

<SettingRow
  label="Submit Key"
  settingKey="general.autoSubmitKey"
  default="Enter"
>
  Which combo to inject: **Enter** works for most chat boxes and forms;
  **Ctrl+Enter** is the convention in IDEs and chat apps (Cursor, ChatGPT
  desktop) where plain Enter inserts a newline. Only shown when Auto-Submit is
  on.
</SettingRow>

### Re-paste the last transcription

Lost the text, or pasted into the wrong window? Re-inject the most recent transcription
without speaking again.

<SettingRow
  label="Re-paste Key"
  settingKey="general.repasteHotkey"
  default="LCtrl+LShift+V"
>
  An **exclusive** global shortcut (it swallows the combo system-wide) that
  re-pastes your last transcription at your cursor. Silent if nothing has been
  transcribed yet. Set it to empty to disable. If it collides with your
  push-to-talk key it's reset to default at startup.
</SettingRow>

## Context-awareness (caret split)

WinSTT can read the focused window's text right before each dictation and feed it to your
LLM cleanup pass, so names and jargon get spelled the way they already appear on screen.
On the dictation path it also reads the text immediately **before and after** your caret
(the caret split), giving the model the sentence it's continuing.

<SettingRow
  label="Context awareness"
  settingKey="general.contextAwareness"
  default="false"
>
  Reads focused-window text through the platform's accessibility APIs immediately
  before each dictation and includes it in the LLM cleanup prompt. Off by default — the
  in-app dialog requires explicit opt-in, since it can read emails, chats,
  editors, and browsers.
</SettingRow>

<Callout type="warn" title="Only used by the LLM cleanup pass">
  Context-awareness does nothing on its own. It requires the **Dictation
  post-processing LLM** to be enabled with a configured model — the captured
  window text is used only in that cleanup prompt, never stored or sent anywhere
  else.
</Callout>

<SettingRow
  label="Context deny-list"
  settingKey="general.contextDenyList"
  default="empty"
>
  Apps and websites that should **never** be read for context. Add executable
  names (e.g. `1password.exe`) or URL hosts (e.g. `bankofamerica.com` —
  subdomains match automatically). On a match, only the harmless window title is
  kept. Password fields are always excluded regardless of this list.
</SettingRow>

<Callout type="success" title="Privacy">
  With context-awareness off (the default), nothing about your screen is read.
  With it on, text goes only to your configured cleanup provider — local Ollama,
  or remote OpenRouter if you chose it. There's no per-app allowlist; the
  deny-list is the override.
</Callout>

## Live-transcription display

The realtime preview is a fast-model approximation that updates as you speak. Choose where
it shows under **Settings → General → Display**.

<SettingRow
  label="Live transcription"
  settingKey="general.liveTranscriptionDisplay"
  default="both"
>
  Where the live preview appears: **In app** (main window only), **In overlay**
  (the floating pill only), **Both**, or **None**. The overlay options require
  the recording overlay to be enabled and the realtime model to be on.
</SettingRow>

The final, pasted text always comes from the **main** model regardless of this setting —
the preview is purely a "WinSTT is hearing you" signal. See
[Real-time preview](/docs/settings/quality) for how the streaming/committed-text window
works.

## Recording overlay

A small pill floats above whatever you're working in while you record, showing an elapsed
timer, the audio visualizer, the live preview, and a thinking indicator. Two layouts are
available.

<SettingRow
  label="Recording Overlay"
  settingKey="general.showRecordingOverlay"
  default="on, xs"
>
  A 6-step slider — **Off**, then five sizes (XS / SM / MD / LG / XL). Controls
  whether the pill shows and how large it is. Greyed out in listen mode.
</SettingRow>

<SettingRow
  label="Overlay Mode"
  settingKey="general.overlayMode"
  default="floating-bottom"
>
  **Floating bottom** (two-piece pill) or **Dynamic island** (docked
  top-center). Greyed out if the overlay is off.
</SettingRow>

<Callout type="info" title="Platform default">
  The overlay defaults to a bottom position on Windows and macOS, and to
  **none** on Linux (compositor quirks). You can override the position to top,
  bottom, auto, or none.
</Callout>
